# doctoral-thesis
Assessment of sea surface salinity variability along Nigerian coastal zone using machine learning – 2012-2021

Abstract

The risks of upstream seawater intrusion from coastal zones (to drinking water supply, people’s
health, sensitive plants like rice and horticultural crops’ yield, and environment) are gradually
becoming serious issues, which require a proactive environmental monitoring and assessment
approach. However, the relevant in-situ, optical and all-weather satellite techniques have both
peculiar and common challenges in sea surface salinity (SSS) observations. Remoteness and large
spatial extent limit the in-situ measurements, seasonal cloud cover affects optical satellite surface
salinity observations, and relatively sparse historical datasets from all-weather satellites discourages
their applications in variability assessment and machine learning (ML) time series prediction. A
common challenge is their inability to support real-time application that can provide the required
early-warning information for mitigating such risks. Consequently, the study determined the annual
and seasonal variability of SSS in the study area from January, 2012 to December, 2021. It assessed
the seasonal surface salinity; and the effects on freshwater availability (FA) in the immediate
maritime area (iMA) and the contiguous Exclusive Economic Zone (cEEZ) from January, 2016 to
December, 2021. The study also determined the relationship between the monthly mean values of
SSS and each of the predictor variables with a ML model. It comparatively assessed ML models for
time series forecasting of monthly mean SSS in the cEEZ. The study adopted experimental research
design and utilized all-weather satellite time series datasets on sea surface salinity (sss), wind speed
(ws), high wind speed (hws), sea surface temperature (sst), absolute dynamic topography (adt), sea
level anomalies (sla), and precipitation (precip) retrieved from appropriate online repositories.
Sequel to the data preparation tasks (data extraction, cleaning and selection), the Microsoft Excel
software was utilized for the descriptive analyses; while the R 4.1.3 in the R-studio 2022.02.3-492
software was utilized to execute the random forest (RF); least absolute shrinkage and selection
operator (LASSO) regression; L0-regularization model (L0); and auto regression integrated moving
average (ARIMA) algorithms for the ML analyses, which involved inferential statistics. The results
of the study show that the annual variability of the mean SSS for the decade under consideration
(2012-2021) is the most stable in terms of the Standard Deviation (SD) of 0.22 psu that is more than
4 times lesser than 1 and Coefficient of Variation (CV) of 0.66% relative to the annual wet season
mean SSS, annual dry season mean SSS and seasonal SSS variability in descending order of
stability. The results also show that the average absolute seasonal SSS (30.51 psu) in the iMA was
relatively lower than that of the cEEZ (33.06 psu) by 2.55psu, which implies that the seasonal
surface water in the cEEZ is about 8.17% (2.55 psu) saltier than that of the iMA on the average. The
average annual and seasonal percentage changes in the SSS in the cEEZ were characterized by
negative (decreased) values, which have similar implications, net positive freshwater flux
(predominance of FA); relatively high freshwater discharge from the tidal rivers; and low risk of
upstream seawater intrusion. These depict an enabling environment for growing rice and
horticultural crops. The result of the comparative assessment of the ML LASSO (RMSE: 0.7428;
MAPE: 1.9039%) and ML ARIMA (RMSE: 0.9850; MAPE: 2.7670%) models’ accuracy for time
series forecasting of the monthly mean SSS in relation to the observed SSS (January-December,
2021) in the cEEZ shows that the two models have high prediction accuracy. However, the former
performed relatively better by a margin of about 24.59% based on the percentage difference in their
RMSE. Despite the observed relatively low risk of upstream seawater intrusion, the ML output of
this study should be updated and adopted by the stakeholders (particularly government agencies and
aquatic entrepreneurs) as an early-warning decision support tool that would enable them to provide
proactive and sustainable preventive measures to any current and future risks of such intrusion.
